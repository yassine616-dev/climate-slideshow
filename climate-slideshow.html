// 00 — Background
What is a
Tipping
Point?
A tipping point is a threshold in Earth's climate system. Cross it, and the change becomes self-sustaining. It no longer needs us to keep going. It feeds itself.

This is what makes climate tipping points different from ordinary environmental problems. Most problems stop when we stop causing them. Tipping points do not. Once triggered, they run.

The debate around climate change often ignores this entirely.

01
Arctic Sea Ice
⚠ Triggered at ~1.5°C
🧊
What is it?
Sea ice is white. It reflects roughly 85% of incoming sunlight back into outer space. How about the water below? It absorbs around 94% of that sunlight. Every time Arctic ice melts, the result replaces what was lost with something capable of absorbing heat rather than reflecting it. The melting process speeds up. More ocean surface exposed. Increased heat absorption. Repeat the process endlessly.
If crossed...
No turning point possible. For the first time in 3 million years, an ice-free Arctic summer may become a reality. Not in the distant future but in this generation according to scientists. Once the self-sustaining cycle gains momentum, it will feed on itself.
Chain reaction — triggers
  • Melt rate at Greenland reaches incredible heights
  • Freshwater flow causes major changes in Atlantic currents
  • Methane emissions begin from permafrost in Siberia & Canada
⚡ Science connection
This is a classic positive feedback loop. We studied one during the class, and here it is, a real-life example. A change creates a response, which strengthens the original trigger. Albedo figures are no mere numbers; they are responsible for the much higher Arctic warming rate than anywhere else.
02
Greenland Ice Sheet
⚠ Triggered at ~1.5°C
🏔️
What is it?
Greenland is holding about 7 metres of sea level rise hostage in frozen form. It's been losing ice every single year since the 1990s. What makes this one especially dangerous is that as the ice melts, the surface drops to lower, warmer elevations which speeds up the melt which drops the surface further. It feeds itself.
If crossed...
Every coastal city on Earth changes permanently. Vancouver, New York, Tokyo, London all partially underwater, not in a disaster movie way, but in a slow, irreversible, generations-long way. There's no engineering solution big enough to stop 7 metres of sea level rise.
Chain reaction — triggers
  • 6-7 metres of eventual sea level rise, locking in centuries of flooding
  • Freshwater input shuts down the Atlantic current
  • Weather patterns across the entire Northern Hemisphere shift
⚡ Science connection
The same albedo effect from the Arctic unit applies here, combined with thermal energy and elevation as the surface lowers into warmer air, it melts faster. That's three Grade 10 concepts stacked into one feedback loop.
03
Amazon Rainforest
⚠ Triggered at ~3-4°C
🌿
What is it?
Many view the Amazon rainforest as being under attack from climate change. What they don't realize is that the forest is not only a casualty but a contributor too. This is due to the fact that the Amazon generates its own rain. How? Trees pull water from the ground and release it as moisture into the air. That moisture rises, forms clouds, and falls as rain. Then the trees drink it and the cycle repeats. Cut down enough trees, or dry out the region with rising heat, and the cycle breaks. The forest stops making rain. Then more trees die. Then more rain stops.
If crossed...
The largest tropical rainforest on Earth converts to dry savannah. About 150-200 billion tonnes of stored carbon get released into the atmosphere roughly a decade's worth of all human emissions dumped at once. Every other tipping point on this map gets hit at the same time.
Chain reaction — triggers
  • Catastrophic CO₂ release accelerates global warming across the board
  • Roughly 10% of Earth's total biodiversity gone
  • South American rainfall collapses affecting agriculture for hundreds of millions
⚡ Science connection
The Amazon is a living carbon sink a direct application of the carbon cycle and photosynthesis from class. It also connects to ecosystem energy flow: when the producers collapse, everything built on top of them collapses too.
04
West Antarctic Ice Sheet
⚠ May already be crossed
🌊
What is it?
Typically, ice sheets reside upon land above sea level. This one does not. The West Antarctic Ice Sheet sits atop bedrock that lies beneath the ocean surface, which means warm seawater can slip underneath it and eat away at it from below. There is no shoreline acting as a barrier. No natural wall to hold it back. On top of that, the bedrock slopes inward, meaning as the edges melt and retreat, they expose even more ice to warm water. The further it retreats, the faster it goes. It is a system designed by geography to fail once it starts.
If crossed...
There's a good chance that this one has already been crossed. If that's the case, then it's only a matter of time before 3-5 meters worth of sea level increase comes about. These are changes that won't be making headlines due to their gradual nature; however, they are irreversible.
Chain reaction — triggers
  • 3-5 meters of guaranteed sea level increase over the centuries to come
  • Furthers destabilization of Greenland Ice Sheet
  • Changes to global ocean circulation impacting global weather
⚡ Science connection
Heat transfer and convection currents in the oceans will cause this phenomenon to occur. The warming waters are less dense and thus rise up and move around the sheet. This involves thermodynamics and fluid mechanics concepts taught in Grade 10.
05
Ocean Currents in the Atlantic
⚠ Weakening now
🌀
What is it?
The Atlantic Ocean contains a massive conveyor belt. Warm surface water travels north, where it cools, becomes denser and saltier, sinks to the ocean floor, and flows back south as deep cold water. This loop has been running for thousands of years and it controls the climate of entire continents. It is the reason Western Europe is warm enough to be populated the way it is. It influences when monsoons happen in Africa and South Asia. It shapes weather patterns across North America. And it is already slowing down. The last time it significantly weakened was 12,000 years ago and parts of Europe dropped 10 degrees Celsius in under a decade.
If crossed...
Temperatures could drop by 5-10 degrees in Europe while other places are heating up. Levels of the ocean on the US East Coast would rise by an additional metre. Climate in areas with monsoons (on which billions of people rely for food and freshwater) would change unpredictably.
Chain reaction — triggers
  • Temperature drops 5-10°C in Europe in decades
  • Less rainfall in the Amazon, risking rainforest collapse
  • Disruption of monsoon systems in Africa and South Asia
⚡ Science connection
AMOC works via thermohaline circulation (water movement due to differences in temperature and salt content affecting density). Thermohaline circulation, density, and convection currents are Grade 10 science curriculum topics.
// 08 — Conclusion
The Domino
Effect is
Already
in Motion
At just 1.1°C of warming, we are already within the lower threshold of some of these tipping points. These are not distant future problems. They are present-tense physics. The science is not debated among climate scientists. What is debated is how fast, and whether we act before the dominoes fall on each other.
